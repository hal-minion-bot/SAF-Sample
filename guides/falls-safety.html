Falls & Home Safety for Ontario Seniors

One in four Canadians over 65 falls each year. Most falls happen at home. This guide shows you how to identify risk factors, make your home safer this week, and get professional help when you need it.

Understanding Fall Risk

Falls aren't random accidents. They result from a combination of factors. The more factors present, the higher the risk. Understanding these helps you target interventions where they'll have the most impact.

Common Fall Risk Factors

  • Medications: Blood pressure medications (especially if blood pressure drops too quickly), sedatives, pain medications, and blood thinners all increase fall risk. If recently started a new medication and balance feels off, talk to your GP.
  • Vision problems: Uncorrected vision, cataracts, macular degeneration, and diabetic retinopathy all increase falls. Get eyes checked yearly. Wear glasses consistently.
  • Footwear: Slippers, loose shoes, and high heels are fall hazards. Wear non-slip, well-fitting shoes with good ankle support.
  • Home hazards: Loose rugs, poor lighting, clutter in walkways, and inadequate grab bars in bathrooms account for 50% of falls in the home.
  • Cognitive decline: Dementia, confusion, and memory loss increase falls because seniors forget hazards or can't adjust to obstacles.
  • Previous falls: If someone has fallen once, they're much more likely to fall again. The fear of falling also changes balance and movement patterns.
  • Muscle weakness: Legs weaken with age. Strength training and movement help prevent falls.
  • Hearing loss: Balance is connected to inner ear function. Untreated hearing loss affects balance and spatial awareness.

Quick Fixes You Can Do This Week

You don't need to hire a contractor to make your home safer. Most of these improvements cost under $50 and take an afternoon.

Bathroom (Where Most Falls Happen)

  • Grab bars in shower/tub: Install grab bars (not towel bars, which can't support weight) beside the shower and tub. Cost: $15-40. Use the studs in the wall for secure mounting.
  • Non-slip shower mat: Wet surfaces are slippery. A non-slip mat costs $10-20. Remove the mat after use to prevent tripping.
  • Shower chair or stool: For those with balance problems, sitting to shower is safer than standing. Cost: $30-50.
  • Toilet seat with armrests: Easier to sit down and stand up safely. Cost: $25-60.

Bedroom and Pathways

  • Remove throw rugs: Tripping hazard. If a rug is essential, use non-slip padding underneath.
  • Clear pathways to bathroom: Declutter hallways. No boxes, newspapers, or cords in walkways.
  • Nightlights: Seniors often need bathroom visits at night. LED nightlights (motion-activated, cost $10-15) prevent stumbling in the dark.
  • Bedside table: Keep water, phone, medications, and reading glasses within arm's reach. Prevents getting out of bed in the dark to look for things.

Lighting

  • Stairway lighting: All stairs need bright lighting. Install lights at the top and bottom. Cost: $20-40 per fixture.
  • Entryway lighting: Outside the front door and in the entry. Makes transitions safer.
  • Eliminate shadows: Dark corners are hazards. Add lamps or adjust existing lights.

Kitchen and Stairs

  • Move frequently used items: Store dishes, glasses, and food at waist height. No reaching or bending.
  • Stair railings: Both sides of staircases should have sturdy railings. Ensure railings are securely fastened.
  • Edge marking: Paint the edges of stair treads white or bright yellow. Helps with depth perception.

Footwear and Assistive Devices

  • Proper shoes: Non-slip soles, good ankle support, laces or velcro (not slip-ons). Cost: $50-100 for quality shoes.
  • Cane or walker: If balance is unsteady, use an assistive device. Using it consistently prevents falls more than refusing to use it out of pride.
  • Walking stick for stairs: If climbing stairs is difficult, a sturdy cane or stair rail helps.
Ontario Assistive Devices Program (ADP): The ADP may cover grab bars, raised toilet seats, bath seats, canes, walkers, and other home safety equipment. To access it:

1. Ask your doctor (MD, nurse practitioner, or specialist) for a referral to an occupational therapist (OT)
2. The OT assesses your home and recommends specific devices
3. The referral and OT assessment are covered under OHIP
4. The ADP covers up to 75% of the cost of recommended devices (up to a specific amount per device)

Many devices you'd buy out-of-pocket are actually eligible for ADP funding. Call 1-800-668-9898 or visit ontario.ca/adp to learn more.

When to Involve a Professional

Occupational Therapist (OT) Home Assessment

An OT can identify hazards you may have missed and recommend modifications. This assessment is especially important if:

  • There's been a recent fall
  • Balance or mobility has noticeably declined
  • The senior is recovering from surgery or hospitalization
  • Cognitive changes have occurred
  • You're unsure what modifications would help most

How to access: Ask your doctor for a referral to an OT. Many are covered under Ontario Health at Home if you're eligible. Private OTs also offer home assessments (typically $200-400 per assessment).

Physiotherapist for Balance and Strength

A physiotherapist can assess strength and balance, teach you exercises to improve both, and recommend when to use assistive devices. Many senior centers and gyms also offer balance and fall prevention classes (often free or low-cost).

GP Conversation About Medications

If falls are recurring, ask your GP: "Are any of my medications contributing to falls? Could we adjust doses or switch to alternatives?" Blood pressure and sedating medications are common culprits. Don't stop taking medications without medical guidance, but definitely bring this up.

Vision Assessment

Get eyes checked yearly after age 65. Corrected vision significantly reduces falls. The Ontario Health insurance plan covers annual eye exams (minus the optometrist's fee).

After a Fall — Immediate Steps

If the Fall Just Happened

  • Don't panic: Take a few deep breaths. Many people who fall can stand up and are fine.
  • Check for injuries: Can they move all limbs? Is there pain? Any obvious bleeding?
  • Don't rush to get up: Attempting to stand quickly after a fall can cause dizziness and another fall. Take time.
  • Call for help if needed: If there's severe pain, inability to move, or head trauma, call 911 immediately.

When to Call 911

Call 911 Immediately If:

  • Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
  • Head injury (even if they seemed okay after, confusion can develop)
  • Severe pain, especially in hip, pelvis, or back
  • Inability to stand or bear weight
  • Signs of internal bleeding (severe bruising, abdominal pain)
  • Chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Severe bleeding that won't stop

How to Help Someone Up Safely

If the fall seems minor and the person can stand with help:

  • Have them roll onto their side
  • Help them get onto hands and knees
  • Have them crawl to a sturdy chair or piece of furniture
  • Using the chair for support, help them stand up slowly
  • Have them sit down for a few minutes to recover
  • Even if they seem okay, contact their doctor to report the fall

Medical alert devices: For seniors living alone or at high risk of falls, consider a medical alert device (pendant or wristband) that lets them call for help if they fall and can't get up. Cost: $25-50/month.

The 'Get Up' Protocol — Helping a Senior Rise Safely

If a senior has fallen and can't get up, the process takes time. Don't rush it — haste causes injury.

  1. Check for injuries: Ask where it hurts. If they report hip, back, or severe pain, call 911 instead of attempting to help them up.
  2. Roll to side: Have them roll onto their side (the less injured side if possible). This is more stable than lying on their back.
  3. Get to hands and knees: From their side, help them get onto hands and knees. This is a more stable position.
  4. Crawl to furniture: Have them crawl (yes, crawl) to a sturdy piece of furniture — a chair, bed, or couch that won't slide or tip.
  5. Use furniture to stand: With hands on the furniture, they push up to kneeling, then to standing. You support their body weight.
  6. Sit down immediately: Once standing, have them sit down right away. Let them rest for several minutes.
  7. Assess: Once seated, check for injuries again. Any pain, dizziness, or confusion?
  8. Report to doctor: Even if they seem fine, have the doctor check them out or at least hear about the fall.

When It's Time for More Support

Sometimes home modifications and lifestyle changes aren't enough. Watch for these signs that it's time to consider additional support:

  • Repeated falls: More than one fall in a 6-month period suggests a pattern that needs professional assessment.
  • Fear of falling: When fear prevents someone from doing normal activities (leaving the house, using stairs), it's time to get help.
  • Can't manage alone: If they're unable to live safely without constant supervision, assisted living or care support may be necessary.
  • Cognitive decline: If they forget about hazards or can't remember safety instructions, they may need more support.
  • Mobility significant decline: When walking becomes very difficult, a walker or mobility aid is essential. Professional PT can help.

Have a conversation with your senior about these changes. Frame it not as loss of independence, but as being smart about getting support so they can stay safe and continue doing things they enjoy.
