Finding Home Care in Ontario

The Ontario care system is confusing. Public vs private? What's covered? How long are wait times? This guide untangles the options, explains real costs, and shows you how to get started today.

When a senior can no longer fully manage at home — due to illness, recovery, or aging — the family faces a complex question: What kind of care? Where? What will it cost? How do I arrange it?

In Ontario, the answer isn't simple. There's publicly funded home care (Ontario Health at Home), private agencies, retirement homes, and long-term care facilities. Each has different costs, eligibility requirements, and timelines. This guide cuts through the confusion.

Understanding Your Options

Ontario Health at Home (Public, Funded)

What it is: Publicly funded home care services provided through Ontario Health (formerly called CCAC — Community Care Access Centre). Services are free or low-cost based on income.

What's covered: Nursing care, personal support (bathing, grooming, toileting), physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietary consultation, wound care, catheter care, and palliative care. Services are coordinated by a care coordinator assigned to you.

Who's eligible: Ontario residents with a valid OHIP card and a documented care need assessed by Ontario Health.

Cost: Free for most people (covered by OHIP). Some higher-income seniors may have a co-pay ($25-50/month depending on service). No charge for nursing assessment or care coordination.

Private Home Care Agencies

What they are: Private companies that employ personal support workers (PSWs) and provide care at your home. No government funding (you pay directly).

Who uses them: People who need care immediately (Ontario Health wait times can be weeks), need more hours than Ontario Health provides, want choice in timing and workers, or have specific preferences not available through public care.

Cost: Typically $25-40/hour for basic personal support in Ontario (2024 rates). 4 hours/day = $100-160/day. Agencies may charge weekly minimums (e.g., 10 hours/week minimum).

Major Ontario agencies: CarePartners, Bayshore Home Health, ParaMed, ComForCare, Victorian Health Services, Nurses On Call. Many communities also have smaller local agencies.

Retirement Homes (Private, Assisted Living)

What they are: Private residential communities for seniors with support services on-site (meals, medication management, activities, some personal care).

Not government subsidized: You pay directly. Cost includes accommodation, meals, and base services.

Cost: $2,500-6,000/month depending on unit size and location. Higher in urban areas (Toronto, Ottawa). Add extra fees for additional care (personal support).

Best for: Seniors who want community, social activities, and support services in one place; those unable to live alone safely; those who want to move before care becomes critical.

Long-Term Care (LTC) Homes (Nursing Homes)

What they are: Licensed facilities providing 24/7 nursing care, personal support, meals, activities, and medical supervision. Government-regulated; some are public, some private.

Who needs them: People with complex medical needs requiring round-the-clock nursing care; those unable to be cared for at home even with support; people with advanced dementia.

Cost: Government-subsidized (OHIP covers nursing and care; resident pays accommodation fee). Accommodation fee: $0-145/day depending on room type and facility. Private LTC homes charge more.

Wait times: Average 2-5 years in Ontario (longer in urban areas). You can be on multiple waitlists simultaneously.

Ontario Health at Home Basics:

Ontario Health at Home provides publicly funded home care in Ontario. It's free (covered by OHIP) for most people, but the system is understaffed and overbooked. Average wait times: 2-6 weeks for non-urgent cases. Urgent cases (post-hospital, palliative care) are prioritized and may get services within days.

To access: Your hospital should refer you before discharge, or call 310-2222 (24/7) to self-refer. You'll be screened for eligibility and assigned a care coordinator who will assess your needs and create a care plan.

Reality check: Most people need to bridge the gap between discharge and Ontario Health arrival with private care (or family support). Budget for 1-2 weeks of private care while you wait.

How to Get Ontario Health at Home

The Step-by-Step Process

  1. Referral: Hospital discharge planner refers you, or you self-refer by calling 310-2222 (24/7).
  2. Initial screening: Ontario Health screens you for eligibility over the phone. They ask about your situation, support system, and urgent needs. This takes 5-10 minutes.
  3. Care coordinator assignment: A care coordinator is assigned to you. They will contact you (usually within 2-3 business days) to schedule an in-home assessment.
  4. In-home assessment: The care coordinator visits your home, reviews your medical history, assesses your functional needs (mobility, cognition, self-care abilities), and identifies safety issues.
  5. Care plan creation: Based on the assessment, a care plan is created specifying what services you need, how often, and for how long.
  6. Services begin: Nurses, PSWs, and therapists are assigned. They contact you to schedule first visits.

What to Expect

  • Wait times: Non-urgent: 2-6 weeks. Urgent (palliative care, post-hospital): days to 1 week. Urgent cases are prioritized.
  • Staffing: You'll have regular visits from assigned workers, but continuity isn't guaranteed. Staff turnover is high; you may have different PSWs on different days.
  • Schedule flexibility: Ontario Health schedules are set by the organization. You have some input, but limited flexibility compared to private agencies.
  • Hours available: Most regions provide weekday daytime hours. Evening, night, and weekend coverage varies by region and need.
  • Communication: Your care coordinator checks in regularly but isn't on-site. If problems arise, call your care coordinator or the Ontario Health office.

Tips for a Smooth Process

  • Self-refer early if possible: Don't wait for hospital referral. Call 310-2222 as soon as you know you'll need care.
  • Be clear about your situation: The more detailed you are during screening, the more accurately they can assess urgency and need.
  • Ask about wait times: When you're assigned a care coordinator, ask specifically how long the wait is for your region and care type.
  • Plan to bridge the gap: Most people need private care while waiting for Ontario Health. Budget accordingly.
  • Ask about additional services: If Ontario Health can't meet all your needs, ask what else they can provide or refer you to (therapy, equipment rental, etc.).

Bridging the Gap with Private Care

When to Consider Private Care

  • Immediate care need (discharge from hospital, sudden health decline)
  • Ontario Health wait times are too long for your situation
  • You need more hours than Ontario Health provides
  • You prefer choice in timing, worker assignments, or specific services
  • Specific care needs (dementia care, specialized personal support)
  • Weekend or evening hours when Ontario Health isn't available

How to Evaluate a Private Care Agency

  • Ask about training: Are PSWs certified? Do they have dementia training? First aid? CPR?
  • Background checks: Do they do police record checks and reference checks? Essential for safety.
  • Worker continuity: Can you request the same worker regularly? Continuity improves care quality.
  • Supervision: Does a supervisor check in? Visit your home? How are complaints handled?
  • Flexibility: Can they accommodate schedule changes? What happens if your assigned worker is sick?
  • Insurance: Are they insured? What if your senior is injured during care?
  • References: Ask for client references. Call at least 2 and ask about their experience.

Questions to Ask Any Care Provider

  1. What specific services do you provide? (personal support, medication management, meal prep, etc.)
  2. What are your hourly rates? Are there minimums (e.g., 4-hour minimum)? Cancellation fees?
  3. How quickly can you start service? (same day, next week, weeks out?)
  4. Can I request the same worker? How do you handle worker continuity?
  5. What happens if my assigned worker calls in sick?
  6. Are your workers trained in dementia care? Palliative care? (if applicable)
  7. Do you do background checks and reference checks on staff?
  8. What's your process for addressing concerns or complaints?
  9. Are you insured? What coverage do you have?
  10. Can you provide references from current or former clients?
Warning: Unlicensed Home Care Agencies

In Ontario, there is no mandatory licensing requirement for private home care agencies. This means many agencies operate without government oversight. Be cautious. Ask:

• Are you licensed? (Not required, but good sign if they are)
• Do you carry liability insurance?
• Can you provide proof of background checks on all staff?
• Are PSWs certified through an Ontario training program?

Choose agencies with strong reputations, verifiable references, and transparent policies. Word-of-mouth recommendations from trusted sources are valuable.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Waiting too long to look for care: Care arrangements take time. Start the process before you're desperate.
  2. Not understanding Ontario Health at Home eligibility: You must be assessed by Ontario Health to access services. Self-referring early means you know if you're eligible and when services will start.
  3. Not bridging with private care: Assuming Ontario Health will be available immediately sets up families for crisis. Plan for a 2-6 week wait and fill gaps with private care if possible.
  4. Accepting the first option: Get quotes from multiple agencies. Compare services, costs, and fit. Don't settle.
  5. Not asking about respite care: If you're a primary caregiver, Ontario Health may provide respite (periodic relief care) to prevent caregiver burnout. Always ask.

Understanding Long-Term Care in Ontario

When It's Appropriate

LTC homes are for people who require 24/7 nursing care and supervision — typically those with:

  • Advanced dementia requiring constant monitoring
  • Multiple complex medical conditions needing round-the-clock nursing
  • Inability to manage personal care even with support
  • Safety risks that can't be managed at home
  • Palliative (end-of-life) care needs

Getting on the Waitlist

You apply through Ontario Health. Your GP or a hospital social worker can initiate the process. You're assessed to determine appropriateness for LTC, assigned a priority level, and placed on a waitlist. You can apply to multiple homes simultaneously to increase chances of placement.

Wait Times and Reality

  • Average wait: 2-5 years in Ontario (longer in urban areas like Toronto)
  • Priority levels: "Most urgent" cases (palliative) move faster. Standard wait is 2+ years.
  • Cost: Government pays for nursing care; you pay room & board ($0-145/day for government-funded homes, more for private)
  • Reality: Many families plan for home care as a long-term solution because LTC wait times are so long

Planning for Costs — A Realistic Guide

Ontario Health at Home

  • Assessment: Free (covered by OHIP)
  • Care coordination: Free (covered by OHIP)
  • Nursing and therapy: Free or low co-pay ($0-25/month for low-income seniors)
  • Personal support: Free to low-income; may be $25-50/month for higher-income seniors
  • Realistic cost: $0-100/month for most people. Higher-income seniors may pay more.

Private Home Care (Hourly Rates, 2024)

  • Personal support (PSW): $25-40/hour
  • Registered nurse (RN): $50-80/hour
  • Common scenario: 4 hours/day, 5 days/week = 20 hours/week at $30/hour = $600/week ($2,400/month)
  • Agency minimums: Most require 10-20 hours/week minimum
  • Variable costs: Weekend and evening hours typically cost more

Retirement Homes

  • Range: $2,500-6,000/month depending on location and unit
  • Toronto/Ottawa: $3,500-6,000/month
  • Regional Ontario: $2,500-4,000/month
  • Included: Accommodation, meals, base activities, medication management
  • Extra fees: Additional personal care, specialized services, outings

Long-Term Care Homes

  • Nursing care: Fully covered by province (OHIP)
  • Room & board: $0-145/day for government-funded homes ($0-4,350/month)
  • Private LTC homes: Higher room & board costs (varies widely)
  • Reality: More affordable than private retirement homes, but long wait times

Sample Scenarios

Scenario 1: Post-hospital discharge, 3 weeks until Ontario Health starts

  • 10 hours/week private PSW: 3 weeks x $300 = $900
  • Ontario Health at Home after 3 weeks: $0-50/month
  • Total cost: $900 + ongoing minimal cost

Scenario 2: Ongoing care need, private care preference

  • 20 hours/week private PSW: $600/week = $2,400/month
  • Ongoing, no wait times, flexible scheduling

Scenario 3: Move to retirement home

  • $3,500/month for accommodation, meals, base support
  • Additional care: $200-500/month extra
