What to Do After a Hospital Discharge in Ontario

Coming home from the hospital is a critical transition. The first 72 hours set the tone for recovery. This guide walks you through exactly what to do, who to contact, and what warning signs mean you need to act fast.

The First 24 Hours

The hours after discharge are when most complications and readmissions happen. Your job is to set up the environment for safe recovery and catch problems early.

Medication Reconciliation (Do This First)

This is the single most important task. Medication errors are the #1 preventable cause of hospital readmission in Ontario.

  • Get the hospital discharge list: The hospital should give you a written list of all medications to take at home — including dose, frequency, and what each one does. If they didn't, ask for one before leaving.
  • Compare to previous medications: Pull out all old medications from before the hospital visit. Which ones are still needed? Which are being stopped? Which are new?
  • Call the pharmacy immediately: Tell them about the hospital discharge list. They'll flag any dangerous interactions or duplicates. (Some people go home on two blood pressure meds by accident.)
  • Take a photo of the discharge list: Upload it to your phone or email it to yourself. You'll need it for follow-up appointments.
  • Set up a pill organizer: If the senior has more than 2 medications, use a weekly pill box. Mistakes happen when there are too many pills.

Follow-Up Appointment with GP

  • Book it in the hospital before discharge. Your GP should see you within 1-2 weeks. Don't leave this to chance.
  • Write down the hospital visit summary: Why were they admitted? What was treated? What was the diagnosis? Take this to the GP appointment.
  • Make a list of questions: Are all these medications necessary? What restrictions apply (diet, activity)? When can they resume normal activities?

Home Setup for Safe Recovery

  • Clear pathways: Make sure hallways, bathrooms, and bedrooms are clear of tripping hazards. No loose rugs, no clutter.
  • Bathroom prep: Put a non-slip mat in the shower. Consider a shower chair or grab bar if mobility is limited. Keep frequently used items at waist height (no bending or reaching).
  • Bedside setup: Water, phone, medications, and a nightlight should all be within arm's reach. Bedside commode if bathroom access is difficult.
  • Temperature control: Keep the home at a comfortable temperature. Recovering seniors are sensitive to temperature changes.
  • Nutrition prep: Stock easy-to-prepare foods. Ensure adequate protein for recovery. Have water readily available.

Who to Call and When

  • Immediate (within 24 hours): Call Ontario Health at Home if a referral was made. Confirm they have your contact info and that a care coordinator will call you.
  • Within 48 hours: Call your GP to confirm the follow-up appointment was booked.
  • If something feels wrong: Don't wait for an appointment. Call the GP's office, your local urgent care, or 911 if it's serious.

The First Week

The first week is about monitoring recovery, getting professional help in place, and catching problems early. Most readmissions happen in the first 7 days.

Daily Monitoring

  • Temperature: Take temperature morning and evening. Fever (over 38°C / 100.4°F) can signal infection.
  • Wound care: If there's a surgical wound, check it daily for redness, swelling, warmth, or discharge. Keep it clean and dry.
  • Mental state: Watch for confusion, disorientation, or personality changes. These are often the first sign something is wrong in seniors.
  • Appetite and hydration: Is the senior eating and drinking normally? Dehydration and poor nutrition slow recovery.
  • Pain level: Pain is normal after surgery, but if it's getting worse instead of better, call the doctor.

Ontario Health at Home Referral

What it is: Ontario Health at Home (formerly CCACs/LHINs) provides publicly funded home care services — nursing visits, personal support workers, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and more. It's free to eligible Ontarians.

How to get it: The hospital should refer you before discharge. If they didn't, call 310-2222 (available 24/7) to self-refer. You'll be screened for eligibility and assigned a care coordinator.

What to expect: A care coordinator will contact you (usually within 2-3 business days) to assess your needs and create a care plan. Wait times vary by region. Non-urgent cases might wait 2-6 weeks for first visit. Urgent cases are prioritized.

What's covered: Nursing care, wound dressing, medication injections, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and personal support (help with bathing, grooming, toileting). Most services are covered by OHIP.

Medication Compliance

  • Take medications exactly as prescribed. Don't skip doses because of cost or side effects.
  • If side effects are intolerable, call the doctor — there are usually alternatives.
  • Use a pill organizer or ask Ontario Health at Home to arrange medication delivery services (if eligible).

Nutrition and Mobility

  • Protein: Recovery requires protein. Include protein at every meal (eggs, yogurt, cheese, fish, chicken, beans).
  • Gentle movement: Bed rest can actually slow recovery. Light walking (even just around the house) helps prevent complications like blood clots and pneumonia.
  • Physiotherapy: If recommended, do the exercises. They're designed specifically for your recovery.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Not filling prescriptions: Some people assume the hospital gave them all medications in discharge papers. You need to pick them up from the pharmacy. Not doing so leads to 30% of readmissions.
  2. Not following up with GP: Skipping the GP appointment means missed opportunities to catch complications early. Always go.
  3. Not calling Ontario Health at Home: Waiting passively for a referral can add 1-2 weeks to your care timeline. Call 310-2222 to self-refer if the hospital didn't.
  4. Ignoring warning signs: Seniors often downplay symptoms ("It's probably nothing"). Trust your instincts. When in doubt, call the doctor.
  5. Trying to manage alone: Pride is expensive. Ask for help. Use available services. Accept offers of support from family and friends.
  6. Rushing back to normal activities: Recovering from hospitalization takes time. Overexertion can trigger setbacks.

Call 911 or Return to Emergency If You Notice:

  • Chest pain or pressure
  • Shortness of breath that's worse than usual
  • Confusion, severe disorientation, or personality changes
  • Wound signs: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, drainage, or opening
  • Fever over 38.5°C (101.3°F)
  • Uncontrolled vomiting or inability to keep medications down
  • Signs of blood clots: severe leg swelling, calf pain, warmth in one leg
  • Severe pain that medications don't control

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I can't get a GP appointment quickly?

Call your GP's office and tell them you were just discharged from the hospital. Most offices will fit you in within 1-2 weeks. If they can't, try a walk-in clinic or urgent care. In Ontario, you can also call Telehealth Ontario at 1-866-797-0000 (24/7) to talk to a nurse for free.

What does Ontario Health at Home actually cover?

Services covered depend on your needs assessment. Most cover: nursing care (wounds, medications), personal support (bathing, grooming), physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietitian consultation, and some specialized services. Cost depends on your income — many low-income seniors pay nothing. There's no single charge; it's covered under OHIP as part of your provincial health insurance.

How do I know if the discharge was premature?

Most hospital discharges are appropriate. But you should worry if: (1) The senior is still in severe pain, (2) Wound care is too complex for home, (3) They can't manage medications, (4) There's no one to provide care at home. If any of these apply, call the hospital's discharge planner or your GP immediately. You can request readmission if truly necessary.
